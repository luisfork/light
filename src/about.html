Understanding Texas Electricity

Everything you need to know to save money on your electricity bill

How Texas Electricity Actually Works

Texas operates the nation's only competitive retail electricity market, where over 300 Retail Electric Providers (REPs) compete for customers across 173 deregulated counties. Understanding this three-layer structure is essential to choosing the right plan.

The Three Players

  • ERCOT (Electric Reliability Council of Texas) manages the grid and wholesale electricity market, serving 85-90% of Texas load.
  • REPs (Retail Electric Providers) purchase wholesale electricity and sell retail plans. They set your rates, handle billing, and provide customer service.
  • TDUs (Transmission and Distribution Utilities) own the physical infrastructure (poles and wires). They charge regulated delivery fees that appear on every bill regardless of which REP you choose.

The Six Major TDUs

  • Oncor - Dallas-Fort Worth area (7.5 million customers, largest)
  • CenterPoint Energy - Houston and surrounding areas
  • AEP Texas Central - Corpus Christi and Rio Grande Valley
  • AEP Texas North - Abilene and San Angelo
  • Texas-New Mexico Power (TNMP) - Scattered areas including Galveston
  • Lubbock Power & Light - Lubbock (joined deregulation in January 2024)
Note: Major cities like Austin, San Antonio, and El Paso remain regulated with municipal utilities and do not participate in the deregulated market.

Why Fixed-Rate Plans Win for Most Texans

Fixed-rate plans lock your price per kilowatt-hour for the entire contract duration (typically 6 to 36 months). They consistently outperform alternatives for three key reasons:

Price Predictability

Fixed rates shield you from market volatility. During Winter Storm Uri in February 2021, variable-rate plans spiked into thousands of dollars per kWh, bankrupting some customers. Fixed-rate customers were protected.

Lowest Overall Costs

Despite marketing claims, fixed-rate plans typically cost less annually than variable, indexed, or time-of-use plans for most households.

Budget Stability

Knowing your rate won't change makes financial planning straightforward. You can accurately predict your electricity costs for the entire contract term.

The Primary Drawback: Early Termination Fees

Fixed-rate plans typically charge $150-$300 ETFs if you cancel early. However, Texas law prohibits ETFs if you move outside the provider's service area (with proof of relocation).

Reading the Electricity Facts Label (EFL)

The Electricity Facts Label is your most important tool, mandated by PUCT Substantive Rule §25.475 to standardize plan disclosures. Every EFL must display the average price per kWh at three usage levels:

  • 500 kWh - Low-usage apartments
  • 1,000 kWh - Texas benchmark (median household)
  • 2,000 kWh - Larger homes with heavy AC usage
Critical Insight: These prices include TDU delivery charges. They represent your total expected cost per kWh, not just the energy rate.

Key EFL Sections to Scrutinize

  • Energy Charge: The per-kWh rate for electricity supply
  • Base Charge: Fixed monthly fee regardless of usage ($0-$30)
  • Bill Credits: Discounts conditional on hitting usage thresholds
  • Early Termination Fee: Flat amount or per-month-remaining formula
  • Renewable Content: Percentage from renewable sources (Texas average ~33%)

How to Calculate Your Expected Bill

  1. Multiply energy charge by your kWh usage
  2. Add base charge
  3. Add TDU charges (both fixed and per-kWh components)
  4. Subtract any bill credits you'll actually qualify for
  5. Add local sales tax (if applicable)

The Bill Credit Trap Costs $800+ Annually

An EnergyBot study of over 500 Texas residents found bill credit plans were 34% more expensive per kWh than traditional fixed-rate plans, costing customers an average of $816 more annually.

How Bill Credits Work (And Fail): These plans offer deceptively low advertised rates by providing large credits (typically $100-$125) only if you use exactly 1,000 kWh. Miss that narrow target, and you pay dramatically higher rates.

Why They Fail in Practice

  • Seasonal Variation: Summer consumption often reaches 1,500-2,500 kWh while fall months drop to 800-900 kWh. You'll miss the credit threshold 8-10 months per year.
  • Narrow Windows: Credits typically require usage within a 50 kWh window (e.g., 1,000-1,050 kWh). This is nearly impossible to hit consistently.
  • Penalty Rates: Missing the credit threshold by even 50 kWh triggers effective rates of 22-23¢/kWh vs. 9-10¢/kWh for simple fixed plans.

Free Nights & Weekends Plans

These suffer similar problems. Unless you use more than 65% of electricity during free hours, you'll pay more than a standard fixed-rate plan. Most households achieve only 25-35% off-peak usage.

Daytime rates on "Free Nights" plans run 18-19¢/kWh versus ~9¢ on standard fixed plans.

TDU Charges Add 30-40% to Every Bill

TDU delivery charges are unavoidable and identical across all REPs in your territory. They're regulated pass-through costs approved by the PUCT.

Strategic Shopping Saves 15-25%

Best Months to Shop

April, May, and October offer the lowest rates—15-25% below peak season pricing. Electricity providers compete aggressively during low-demand months.

Worst Months to Shop

July, August, and January see rates spike up to 40% higher due to extreme weather demand.

Contract Expiration Strategy

If your contract expires during peak season, consider an odd-term plan (8, 9, or 14 months) to shift future renewals to favorable months. A 9-month plan starting in January expires in September/October when rates are lowest.

The Holdover Rate Trap: If you do nothing when your contract expires, you're automatically placed on a variable "holdover rate" that's typically 20-50% higher than contracted rates. Most Texans save $200-500 annually simply by switching at renewal.

Critical Timeline

  • REPs must notify you 30 days before contract expiration
  • You can switch within 14 days of expiration without ETF penalty
  • Start shopping 60 days before your contract ends

Consumer Protection Under Texas Law

The Public Utility Commission of Texas (PUCT) enforces consumer protections under the Public Utility Regulatory Act.

Your Rights

  • Clear information via standardized EFLs
  • Protection from unauthorized switching (slamming)
  • Protection from unauthorized charges (cramming)
  • Privacy of customer-specific information
  • Right to switch providers within 14 days of contract expiration without penalty
  • No ETF if you move outside the provider's service area

Filing a Complaint

  1. First, contact your REP to resolve the issue
  2. If unresolved, file with PUCT online at puc.texas.gov/consumer/complaint
  3. Call PUCT at 1-888-782-8477
  4. Mail complaints to PUCT headquarters

Note: PUCT investigates regulatory violations, not general satisfaction issues with customer service.
